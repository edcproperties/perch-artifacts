Worked example

How to read a lead: Holiday Heating.

Provo, UT · DOPL S350 HVAC · Kevin Crickenberger, owner · 20+ yrs on boilers

Serves residential, commercial, and industrial — radiant floors up to “large-scale industrial steam systems.” On their own site: “other contractors often call us when they’re stumped on a boiler issue.” The boiler authority other trades defer to.

S350 HVAC license → Division 23 → §23 52 00 Heating Boilers → their industrial steam brushes the Division 40s (Process Heating)
  • Do they bid commercial? Yes. HVAC is one of only ~4 trades a B100 GC can’t self-perform — so it’s always bid out. Holiday Heating is structurally locked into the invitation-to-bid pain Perch fixes.
  • The insider distinction: building heat = Div 23 (hydronic/heating boilers); industrial process steam = Div 42. Ask which side their work falls on and you instantly read as someone who’s been on a jobsite.

Talk tracks

Drop the code, earn the room.

Goals first — the division talk is seasoning that proves you belong, not the opener. Use it once or twice, then get back to their goals and pain. Every division card above has its own line ready to lift.

Vendor“So… what kind of work do you guys bid on?”
Insider“You’re a Division 23 shop — bidding the mechanical scope straight to GCs, or coming in under a mechanical prime?”
Vendor“Do you get a lot of bid requests?”
Insider“When the GC sends the ITB, is it a clean 23 00 00 section, or are they lumping 22 and 23 so you price plumbing scope that isn’t yours?”
Vendor“How do you pick which jobs to chase?”
Insider“HVAC’s a scope the GC can’t self-perform, so you’re always invited — drowning in invites and cherry-picking, or chasing to keep the pipeline full?”

The reflex to build

Before each call, glance at the DOPL class on the card → say the division out loud once. S280 → “Division 7, roofing.” E200 → “Division 26, electrical.” A week of that and it’s automatic.
