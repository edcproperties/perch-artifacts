Perch · Sales Field Notes · № 001

First Day on the Phones

One rep, one phone, a full day of cold calls into Utah contractors. What connected, what died at the front desk, and what we know now that we didn't at 11:12 AM. Every claim below has a receipt from a call transcript.

Jeremy Palmer · first sales hire · July 2, 2026 · Procore-100 call list, Salt Lake & Utah counties

TL;DR

What we learned

  • The student ask opens doors. 38% of dials reached a human, one brush-off all day, and receptionists turned into advocates.
  • Cell numbers skip gatekeepers entirely. Zero screens on 17 cell dials, and both owner conversations came off the flagged-cell list.
  • Say "estimating" or "bidding." "Pre-con" confused sub-side front desks twice and triggered the day's only brush-off.
  • The gold is after the goodbye. Roughly 35 of the best call's 45 minutes came after the wrap attempt.
  • Bad plans and vague specs are the market's #1 pain. Four companies confirmed it independently in one day.
  • Windows decide outcomes. Conversation rate before 4:30 was 21%; after, 6%. The noon hour hit recorded lunch greetings.

Carry into the next call day

  • Work the Monday queue first (section H): 16 warm re-entries with names. Brian, Gary, Spencer Nelson, Kelly, Bill or Sean.
  • Exit checklist on every discovery call: full name, direct line or email, and "can I call you again when I've got more questions?"
  • One story. Pick a school and keep every detail identical on every call.
  • Dial estimators 9:00 to 11:45 and 1:00 to 4:00. Save owner cells for off-hours. Skip pre-holiday afternoons.
  • Log at hangup, every time. Twenty seconds while it's fresh beats an hour of reconciliation later.
  • Voicemail policy: nothing on office boxes; a 15-second student line on named personal cells.

B

Two different weapons: office lines and cell numbers

Before the afternoon block we ran a carrier lookup on all 75 numbers in the queue and flagged the 17 that are mobile lines (8 Verizon, 6 T-Mobile, 3 AT&T). The theory: owner-operators publish their cells, so a mobile number should skip the front desk entirely. Day-one evidence says the theory holds, with a nuance worth keeping.

Cell numbers (17 dials)Office lines (30 dials)

The nuance: cells don't raise the connect rate (most of them were dialed after 4:40 PM and went to voicemail like everything else). What they change is who answers when someone answers. Every cell connect was a decision-maker. Every office-line connect went through a screen first, and eleven dials never got past it. Meanwhile the five best estimator conversations (SUNROC, IMS, Hunsaker, Boswell, Burbach) all came through office lines on the student ask. So the play is both: office line + student ask to reach estimators during business hours, flagged cell to reach the owner, any hour.

Standing rule № 1

Dial estimators 9:00 to 11:45 and 1:00 to 4:00. Dial owner cells anytime. Never burn the queue between noon and 1:00, after 4:30, or the afternoon before a holiday weekend.

C

The moves that worked, with receipts

C-1

The two-question student ask disarms everyone

The frame ("I'm a student, I have a couple quick questions about how you do estimating, do you have like two seconds?") got through every screen it met except one. Receptionists didn't just pass the call; some became advocates. Jordan at Intermountain Aquatech physically went upstairs looking for her estimator, then wrote a sticky note promising a callback. The only pushback all day was about timing, never about the ask.

I told my front desk this guy has the balls to call here, just cold call and I'll give him some time.Cody, sole estimator, Boswell Millwork (15-minute call)
C-2

Say "estimating" or "bidding." Never say "pre-con" to a sub.

"Pre-con" is GC vocabulary. At Western Fence it confused the gatekeeper into a brush-off; at RC Hunt it got misheard as "prefab" and produced a blind transfer to the wrong employee. The plain ask for the "bidding department" at IMS Masonry produced an instant clean transfer to an estimator.

Commercial pre con? What do you mean by that? … We're not interested.Megan, front desk, Western Fence, before the recovery in C-3
C-3

The first "no" is soft. Re-anchor once.

Megan said "we're not interested" and was one breath from hanging up. Re-anchoring on the student frame ("I'm just a student, literally two questions") flipped her into transferring the call and volunteering the commercial estimator's full name, Spencer Nelson. One polite second attempt, no pushiness, name in the ledger.

Let me see if Spencer's available. Hold on.Megan, same call, ninety seconds later
C-4

Convert every miss into a name

When Gene Peterson's receptionist said the estimators were out, the counter wasn't a message. It was "who should I ask for next time I call?" Answer: Bill or Sean. That question turns a dead dial into a warm re-entry, and it worked everywhere it was used: Kelly at TST, Brian at Wall 2 Wall (plus his return date), Kevin at Aquatech. Seventeen of the 47 companies now have a named target. The one time the free hook was declined (Amber at Schoonover offered to take a message), the callback starts cold again.

Bill or Sean, either one of them.Front desk, Gene Peterson Concrete, in exchange for one question
C-5

Mirror what they said, then chain the next question onto it

The Boswell and Maverix calls stayed alive on one engine: play back what they just told you in your own words, get the "100% correct," then push one layer deeper. Short reflections ("So 10 times." "Chicken and egg problem?") did more work than long questions. It proves you're listening, and experts keep teaching people who are visibly learning.

So it sounds like you guys have a pretty good process through Building Connected… what's that next step?The playback-then-chain pattern, Boswell call
C-6

Spitball naively and let them correct you

"I'm just spitballing here" followed by a half-right guess made Nate correct the record in detail, twice. People can't resist fixing a wrong model of their own business. The correction is always richer than the answer to a direct question would have been.

C-7

The fake goodbye is where the goldmine is

Ten minutes into the Maverix call came the polite wrap: "I didn't want to eat up too much of your time." Nate then talked for another thirty-five minutes, unprompted: pricing calibration, material economics, cash-flow mechanics, the Oracle origin story, his evidence-collection playbook. The same thing happened in miniature with Cody. Once you stop asking, they relax and volunteer the real stuff. When the goodbye triggers a monologue, go silent and take notes.

C-8

Take the downgrade

At Burbach the commercial estimators were gone. Instead of hanging up, the call took the junior residential estimator ("maybe they're a little bit closer to being a student than the other person anyways"). That downgraded call produced the day's most important competitive signal: Burbach already pays for a company-wide AI account with a shared database that auto-generates bids, builds customer profiles, and tracks job costing.

When one employee does something and interacts with it or puts in a pricing sheet or a bid or an estimate, it all goes to the main database.Jonas, estimator, Burbach Companies, on their corporate AI account
D

Anatomy of the best call: 45 minutes with the owner of Maverix

Nate runs a $6M/yr Division-8 door supply company he started two years ago. He answered his own cell (an AT&T number off the flag list), gave a masterclass in his own business, and never once asked who was really calling. This is the call to study. 7,995 words of transcript, 183 speaker turns, roughly 45 minutes.

0:00

"Hello?" No IVR, no receptionist. The cell number was the routing.

0:10

Micro-ask: "Do you have like two seconds?" Two seconds is deniable; it always becomes more.

~2:00

Pain probe gets "Not really, no." A rookie moves on. The follow-up ("what makes a good plan?") cracks it open: door consultants vs. architects who write their own specs and get hardware wrong.

~6:00

Spitballed a half-right theory about specialization. Nate corrects it and, in correcting it, explains how GCs force split bids and cherry-pick scopes.

~10:00

The wrap attempt: "I didn't want to eat up too much of your time." Nate relaunches, unprompted.

10:00 to 44:00

The monologue: a year to dial in pricing. Material is the cost driver ($250 labor vs $2,500 material per door). Brands won't sign up new suppliers. Five door-supply players in Utah. Cash cycle is 3 to 5 months out at all times; manufacturers demand 100% prepay until you earn net-30/60. First job was an Oracle data-center order at 70-80% margin, paid upfront, which seeded the operating float he later grew to ~$700K by billing a disorganized GC 70% up front. And the discipline that protects all of it: evidence collection, "per our conversation" emails after every phone change.

44:00

"I didn't even catch your name. I'm so sorry." Nate. The only thread back, caught at the last second.

Evidence collection is like the difference maker… They're trying to find where you messed up and pin you with it.Nate, owner, Maverix Contracting

Why it worked, in one sentence: a no-threat caller reached a founder directly, mirrored his language, guessed wrong on purpose, and then had the discipline to stay quiet after the goodbye.

What it cost us: no email, no explicit permission to call again. See E-1.

E

What failed, and the fix

E-1 · The two best calls ended with no path back

Boswell ended on pure thanks: no last name, no direct line, no permission to follow up, with the single most valuable discovery contact of the day. Maverix got a first name in the final ten seconds. Fix: a hard exit checklist on every discovery call. First and last name, direct line or email, and one sentence: "Can I call you again when I've got more questions?" Nobody who just talked for 15 minutes says no.

E-2 · The story drifted between schools

Seven calls said "student at the Y," nine said "student up at the U," sometimes within the same hour. Utah construction is a small world and these people talk to each other. Fix: pick one program and one class, and keep every detail of the story stable across the whole list.

E-3 · Eighteen dials were spent when nobody answers the phone

The 4:41-to-5:24 block produced twelve voicemails, one dead line, and one lucky owner connect. The noon hour hit two recorded lunch-closure greetings. That's ~40% of the day's dials aimed at windows where connects are structurally unlikely. Fix: Standing rule № 1, plus save the cell-number list for off-hours blocks, since owners answer their own phones when offices don't.

E-4 · Logging happened in batches, not at hangup

Six duplicate log entries, one call never logged, and the best call of the day logged 63 minutes late. Reconstructing true dispositions for this report required reading transcripts. Fix: log at hangup, every time. The cockpit's draft autosave makes it a 20-second habit; the granola sync backfills the rest.

E-5 · Twenty-one voicemails, zero messages left

Defensible on office lines (a student VM to a department box does nothing). But two of those boxes were owners' personal cells with their names in the greeting (Scott Denny, Lindsey). A 15-second message there warms the redial. Fix: a two-line policy. Office box: hang up, redial another day. Named personal cell: leave the student line and a callback number.
